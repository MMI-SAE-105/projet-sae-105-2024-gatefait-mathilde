To uncover the true history of eyewear, Neil Handley explores their evolution in his book Cult Eyewear.

THE BOOK

Cult Eyewear: The World’s Enduring Classics, written by Neil Handley, delves into the evolution of eyewear and its cultural impact, from the earliest frames to the most contemporary designs.

The book traces the history of iconic glasses that have left a mark, such as the famous Aviator by Ray-Ban or the Wayfarer, while examining their role as fashion accessories and symbols of social status.

Handley presents glasses not just as tools for vision correction but also as cultural and design objects that influence trends.

MODERNITY

The book highlights major brands and designers who have transformed glasses into fashion icons, emphasizing the importance of materials, technical innovations, and collaborations with renowned creators.

Cult Eyewear also explores how celebrities, musicians, and films have popularized certain models, reinforcing the status of glasses as essential accessories.

HANDLEY AND THE FUTURE

Handley does not limit himself to the history of glasses. He also explores their role in the current world and their future prospects.

He discusses technological advances such as smart glasses with augmented reality features and innovative materials that make frames more durable and ergonomic.

These trends show that glasses will continue to evolve, becoming not only tools for vision but also indispensable technological devices in our daily lives.

STRENGTH OF THE BOOK

One of the book’s strengths is highlighting that glasses are much more than functional objects.

They represent a form of personal expression and influence the identity of those who wear them.

The chapters on designer models and the increasing integration of technology into frames illustrate how this accessory has become a crucial element of contemporary fashion and a driver of innovation.

In summary, Cult Eyewear offers an enriching perspective on glasses, covering their past, their role in current fashion, and their future as technological and cultural objects.
